The EU AI Act has been in force since 1 August 2024 and rolls out in phases. The date that matters most for organisations in the Netherlands: **on 2 August 2026 Dutch supervision becomes operational** and most obligations apply in full. This guide lays out what the law asks of you and in which order to arrange it.

## Who does the AI Act apply to?

Short answer: nearly every organisation. The law distinguishes providers (who build or sell AI systems) from deployers (who use AI). If your organisation uses ChatGPT, Copilot or an AI feature inside your software, you are a deployer and obligations apply, regardless of company size.

## The four risk categories

  <ul class="space-y-3 text-stone-600">
    <li><strong class="text-brand-dark">Unacceptable risk (prohibited):</strong> social scoring, emotion recognition in the workplace, manipulative AI. Banned since 2 February 2025.</li>
    <li><strong class="text-brand-dark">High risk:</strong> AI in recruitment, credit scoring, education, critical infrastructure. Heavy requirements: risk management, documentation, human oversight, registration.</li>
    <li><strong class="text-brand-dark">Limited risk:</strong> chatbots and AI-generated content. Transparency obligation: users must know they are dealing with AI.</li>
    <li><strong class="text-brand-dark">Minimal risk:</strong> spam filters, spell checkers. No additional obligations.</li>
  </ul>
</div>

Most SMEs use AI in the limited and minimal risk categories. Watch out for AI in HR processes: a CV screening tool quickly qualifies as high risk.

## The timeline

- **2 February 2025**: prohibited practices in force, plus the AI literacy obligation (Article 4) for everyone using AI.
- **2 August 2025**: rules for general-purpose AI models and the governance structure.
- **2 August 2026**: most remaining obligations, including those for high-risk systems. The Dutch supervision and sanction regime is operational.
- **2 August 2027**: final obligations for high-risk AI in regulated products.

## Five steps to compliance

### 1. Inventory your AI use

Map which AI systems your organisation uses, including the invisible ones: AI features in existing software, tools employees adopted on their own. No inventory, no compliance.

### 2. Classify by risk category

Determine the risk category per system. For most tools that is limited or minimal risk; flag anything touching HR, financial decisions or safety for closer review.

### 3. Arrange the literacy obligation

Article 4 has required sufficient AI literacy among staff working with AI since February 2025. This is the obligation nearly every organisation must already meet. A targeted <a href="/en/services/ai-literacy-training">AI literacy training</a> with documentation makes it demonstrable.

### 4. Document agreements in an AI policy

Which tools are allowed, which data may go in, who is responsible. See our guide to <a href="/en/resources/ai-policy-guide">writing an AI policy</a>.

### 5. Check transparency and high-risk obligations

If you use chatbots or AI-generated content towards customers, arrange the transparency notices. If you (possibly) operate high-risk systems, start the documentation and oversight work now: it takes months, not weeks.
